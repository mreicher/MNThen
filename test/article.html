Cassius, Anthony Brutus (A.B.) (1907 – 1983)

Matt Reicher
Published: November 3, 2025
Updated: June 15, 2026

Anthony Brutus (A.B.) Cassius had a front-row seat to racial injustice in the Twin Cities after arriving from Oklahoma in the early 1920s. He saw, firsthand, how opportunity and dignity were parceled out by color. Rather than retreat, Cassius pushed forward—into business, into politics, and into the heart of the city's segregated economy. Over the next four decades, he would become one of Minneapolis's most visible Black entrepreneurs, labor advocates, and community leaders. His long fight for an on-sale liquor license between 1947 and 1948 was not just a legal victory; it was the public culmination of a life spent demanding a seat at the table.

Cassius began working in Minneapolis hotel kitchens, first at the Merchants Hotel and later at the Curtis Hotel, where he became a union business agent for Hotel Workers Local #614. His years in the hospitality industry exposed him to the racial barriers that shaped who could work in certain positions and who could be served. In 1939, Cassius and his brother opened the Dreamland Café at 3759 Fourth Avenue South, one of the first Black-owned restaurants in the city to hold a beer license. Dreamland became a central gathering place for South Minneapolis's Black community, offering a space where African Americans could socialize without facing discrimination.

As Dreamland grew, Cassius became active in civic organizations. He helped organize the Minnesota Club, a professional association for Black business owners, and served as president of the Nacirema Club, a social venue for the city's Black middle class. He remained involved in the labor movement and supported The Way, a community organization for Black youth.

In 1947, Cassius applied for an on-sale liquor license for his Cassius Bar and Café at 207 Third Street South, an area dominated by white-owned establishments. He met all legal requirements, had passed inspection, and maintained a clean record at Dreamland. Yet the application process stretched to eighteen months, marked by racial opposition and bureaucratic obstruction.

Some aldermen demanded he buy out equipment from a recently closed white bar at significant cost—an unofficial practice that favored established business owners. Cassius refused, arguing there was no legal basis for the requirement. His refusal drew attacks. Anonymous accusations linked him to the Northside "underworld." Others claimed he had ties to the Communist Party because of his labor activism in the 1930s. A neighborhood petition opposed the license, warning it would bring "large numbers of Negroes" to the area.

Even sympathetic city officials wavered under pressure. On August 7, 1947, the City Council denied his request. Cassius spent another year in limbo while the city revised its licensing ordinances. He reapplied in late 1948 and, after relentless effort, prevailed. On December 31, 1948, Anthony B. Cassius became the first Black man in Minneapolis history to hold an on-sale liquor license.

The victory was costly. The delays and appeals drained his savings. Banks refused him credit until the president of Midland Bank personally interceded to secure a $10,000 loan—an extraordinary act of financial trust at a time when most institutions routinely denied Black applicants. But Cassius endured. His downtown bar became an interracial meeting place known for its dignity and polish, where men wore suits and women came in their best dresses. For a generation, it stood as proof that success in a segregated city was possible, even when the odds said otherwise.

Cassius operated two establishments: Dreamland in south Minneapolis and his bar downtown. Both were listed in the Negro Motorist Green Book, providing Black travelers with safe places to stop in the city. His businesses remained open for decades. Cassius Bar and Café closed in 1980, years after he had retired. His work influenced subsequent Black business owners and the civic organizations he helped establish.

Anthony Brutus Cassius died in 1983. His downtown liquor license fight was about more than one man's business—it was a test of whether Black entrepreneurs could claim space in white-dominated commercial districts. City officials opposed him on racial and political grounds, using bureaucratic delays and unwritten rules to block his application. When Cassius finally won his license in December 1948, he demonstrated that determined Black business owners could overcome discriminatory barriers. The victory opened a path for other African American entrepreneurs in Minneapolis who came after him.
