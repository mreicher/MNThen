Rondo Neighborhood and Interstate 94

Desire Lines, Redlining, and the Path of Interstate 94

Matt Reicher December 11, 2025 St. Paul, MN

Quick Facts

  • Alternative ProposedNorthern Route (George Herrold, 1945)
  • Households Displaced600+, mostly from Rondo
  • Businesses Displaced~300
  • Federal Cost Share90% (Federal-Aid Highway Act, 1956)
  • OpenedDecember 9, 1968

Key Dates

  • Nov. 1, 1945Pioneer Press endorses new east–west highway
  • 1947Herrold backs Northern Route alternative

Discussions about building a highway to connect Minneapolis and St. Paul began in 1920 and gained momentum shortly after World War II. Rapidly increasing automobile use meant it was time to address surging gridlock on local streets, and local business groups and traffic engineers pushed for a direct east–west corridor to serve the region's growing commercial districts. When highway department officials locked in on St. Anthony Avenue — straight and familiar, running parallel to University and Marshall from downtown to the western city limit — they followed what they called "desire lines," concluding that St. Anthony was where the traffic already wanted to go. What the traffic maps did not show was a community.

I

Federal Policy and Residential Segregation

Since the 1930s, federal policy and private practice had confined Black families to a narrow corridor of St. Paul. The Home Owners' Loan Corporation had graded neighborhoods by lending risk, producing color-coded maps that marked areas with high concentrations of African American residents in red — the lowest grade, labeled "Hazardous." The Federal Housing Administration reinforced this by restricting mortgage insurance to white-only neighborhoods and encouraging racial covenants. Across the Twin Cities, developers inserted language into property deeds stipulating that premises could not be sold, mortgaged, or leased to anyone other than members of the Caucasian race.

Black families were funneled into a single neighborhood where they could buy homes but could not secure favorable loans or expand into surrounding areas. When highway planners looked at a map in the 1940s, they saw a straight, wide corridor running east to west through St. Paul — and it ran directly through Rondo.

II

The Warning of George Herrold

In 1953, what had long been a threat became fact when Rondo families learned their elementary school would be demolished for a freeway. The route, however, had been effectively locked in for years. Even the city's own planning director, George Herrold — who had held the position since 1920 — recognized St. Paul's pattern of "slash-and-cut" planning and warned that this highway would "decimate" Rondo. He noted that the city was already divided into isolated sections by twenty-two railroad lines, and that adding a freeway through a residential corridor would only cut it further apart.

Herrold warned the project would sever the Capitol complex from downtown and leave behind a "gigantic ditch … and an unwelcome concentrator of exhaust fumes." In 1947, he had backed an alternative called the Northern Route — a four-lane roadway along existing railroad lines near what is now Pierce Butler Route. It would bypass Rondo almost entirely, minimize disruption to Prospect Park, and keep the Capitol tied to downtown, all at the cost of no more than a couple of additional minutes of commute time.

III

Engineering Efficiency vs. Community Integrity

The Northern Route never had a real chance. City officials prioritized maximum traffic capacity, citing studies that placed the bulk of future freeway users south of University Avenue. Minneapolis planners and University of Minnesota administrators further entrenched the southern path, demanding a direct alignment for campus access. Additionally, Herrold's route projected higher traffic volumes on connecting streets — a financial burden of repairs and maintenance the city was unwilling to shoulder.

His proposal also lacked eligibility for federal funding when the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 arrived, meaning the city would have borne the added costs alone. Ultimately, the economic and political case for the St. Anthony alignment held firm, effectively prioritizing engineering efficiency over the integrity of the Rondo community.

IV

Rondo: A City Within a City

What stood in the path of that decision was the heart of Black professional and social life in St. Paul — home to the Appeal newspaper, the Hallie Q. Brown Center, and the Credjafawn Social Club. Black-owned businesses lined Rondo Avenue: barbershops, beauty parlors, restaurants, and the Credjafawn Cooperative Store. It was a mixed-income community where doctors and lawyers lived on the same streets as porters and domestic workers, and where the St. Paul chapter of the NAACP operated.

These businesses and institutions formed a self-contained social and economic infrastructure that the community had built because they were largely excluded from the rest of St. Paul. Rondo was, in essence, a city within a city. The neighborhood's vitality was not incidental to its geography — it was a direct product of the discriminatory policies that had hemmed its residents in for decades.

V

The Federal-Aid Highway Act and the Final Link

President Eisenhower's Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 sealed Rondo's fate. With the federal government agreeing to cover ninety percent of construction costs, the financial calculus that might have kept Herrold's route competitive evaporated entirely. Herrold's Northern Route was never seriously reconsidered. City leaders deviated little from their original plan, and construction stretched across nearly a decade.

On December 9, 1968, a coalition of leaders from both cities met near Highway 280, tied ribbons together in front of roughly two hundred people, and declared the Twin Cities linked. By four o'clock that afternoon, Interstate 94 was open. It had taken more than 600 households and some 300 businesses with it — most of them from Rondo, one of the few St. Paul neighborhoods where Black families had been allowed to settle.

VI

Memory, Reckoning, and What Remains

The community that survived the freeway refused to disappear quietly. In 1983, the inaugural Rondo Days festival was held, establishing an annual celebration of the neighborhood's history and culture that continues to this day. The festival is an act of collective memory — a deliberate, joyful insistence that the neighborhood's identity outlasted the concrete that cut through it.

Decades later, the state began to reckon more formally with what had been lost. In 2016, MnDOT launched "Rethinking I-94," a community-driven planning effort to address the equity and mobility harms created by the original corridor. The conversations that process opened — about land use, reconnecting neighborhoods, and historical accountability — remain ongoing. The freeway is still there. So is Rondo.

Timeline

1920

Early highway discussions begin in the Twin Cities. George Herrold appointed St. Paul's first city planner.

1930s

HOLC redlining maps grade Rondo "Hazardous." FHA mortgage restrictions confine Black families to the neighborhood.

Nov. 1, 1945

Pioneer Press endorses new east–west highway. Highway Dept. settles on St. Anthony Avenue alignment.

1947

Herrold formally backs the Northern Route — along rail lines north of Pierce Butler — as the preferred alternative.

1953

Rondo families learn their elementary school will be demolished for the freeway.

1955

Residents form the Rondo–St. Anthony Improvement Association to oppose the routing.

1956

Federal-Aid Highway Act passed. Federal government to cover 90% of costs. Northern Route loses its last opening.

1958

Final I-94 corridor fixed. Rondo's fate is sealed.

1959

Merriam Park Residential Association forms to fight an elevated freeway design.

1960–1968

Construction of I-94 through Rondo. 600+ households and ~300 businesses displaced.

Dec. 9, 1968

I-94 dedicated. Ribbon-cutting at Hwy 280. Twin Cities officially linked.

1983

Inaugural Rondo Days festival held — an annual celebration of the neighborhood's history and culture.

2016

MnDOT launches "Rethinking I-94," a community-driven effort to address the equity harms of the original corridor.

Key Figures

George Herrold

St. Paul City Planner from 1920 to 1950. One of the region's earliest professional planners and a consistent critic of "slash-and-cut" street widening. Proposed the Northern Route in 1945 to protect Rondo and Prospect Park — a plan that was ultimately ignored.

Dwight D. Eisenhower

President of the United States. His signing of the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 finalized the financial framework that made the St. Anthony alignment inevitable and foreclosed Herrold's alternative.

Rondo Community

The heart of Black professional and social life in St. Paul. Despite displacement, former residents and their descendants have sustained the neighborhood's identity through Rondo Days and ongoing advocacy.
