Erd - Geist Gazebo

Creator: Matt Reicher Published: May 28, 2025

The octagonal Erd-Geist Gazebo represents one of White Bear Lake's finest examples of Victorian craftsmanship. This distinctive structure once stood along the eastern edge of Schneider's Bay, near what was then Schneider's Road (now South Shore Boulevard), and today rests in Matoska Park. Built by master carpenter and stonecutter Thomas Erd—known for his work on St. Paul's Church of the Assumption—the gazebo was constructed in the ornate tradition of Minnesota's Gilded Age lake resorts.

In the summer of 1883, Erd completed the gazebo as a wedding gift for his daughter Anna and her husband, Emil Geist, a jeweler from St. Paul. Positioned prominently on their south shore property, the two-story structure served both as a private retreat and a symbol of the family’s investment in the refined lifestyle emerging in White Bear Lake’s growing resort area. In 1911, the gazebo’s lower section was enclosed so it could be used as a honeymoon cottage for family members.

After sitting for many years in disrepair, and likely facing demolition, the property’s then-owner donated the gazebo to the White Bear Lake Women's Club in 1973. Negotiations with the city provided a new location for the structure at Matoska Park near the Manitou Bridge. However, the club encountered several setbacks as the gazebo’s ownership changed several times in just a few years. These frequent changes in ownership complicated the restoration and relocation process, leading to delays and uncertainties about the gazebo's future.

Once settled, plans were made to transport the structure across the frozen White Bear Lake in February 1974. However, unseasonably thin ice made the crossing too risky, prompting a last-minute decision to truck the hand-carved gazebo—disassembled into two pieces—over local roads to the west side of the lake. The $2,500 cost of the relocation was covered by donations from local banks and other contributors, including $1,000 from the Minnesota Historical Society. With the move complete, restoration work began in anticipation of dedication.

By the late 1990s, deterioration had once again threatened the beloved structure. The city had been forced to dismantle the gazebo two years earlier when rotting wood compromised the stairs, columns, railings, ceilings, and floors. A community fundraising campaign raised $60,000 for restoration, with local resident and former city attorney Ted Glasrud contributing $40,000 of that total. Restoration work began in early June 2000, and the gazebo was rededicated that July 9th.

Two decades later, another major restoration became necessary. By May 2021, the second-story stairs had deteriorated so severely that the city was forced to board them up for safety. This time, the restoration aimed not just to repair but to upgrade materials for greater longevity while preserving the gazebo's original Victorian character. The city allocated $180,000 for the project—$30,000 from its Parks Fund and $150,000 from an anonymous donor working through the Greater White Bear Lake Community Foundation. The completed restoration returned the gazebo to public use on August 14, 2021.

The Erd-Geist Gazebo stands as one of White Bear Lake's most enduring symbols of Gilded Age elegance. What began as a master craftsman's wedding gift to his daughter has evolved into a cherished community landmark, surviving multiple relocations, restorations, and demolition through the persistent efforts of local residents and generous donors. Its Victorian architecture continues to provide a picturesque backdrop for weddings, family photographs, and community gatherings—serving as a distinctive and enduring tribute to the area's resort-era legacy.

Quick Facts

  • Name: Wildwood Amusement Park
  • Location: Southern shore of White Bear Lake, Mahtomedi, MN
  • Opening Year: 1889
  • Initial Operator: Minneapolis and St. Paul Suburban Railroad Company
  • Main Attractions: Dance hall, roller coaster, merry-go-round, tilt-a-whirl, Ferris wheel, shooting gallery, penny arcade
  • Notable Activities: Hot-air balloon rides, baseball on beach, traveling shows, boating, and swimming
  • Renamed: White Bear Park in 1916
  • Final Closing: 1932
  • Reason for Decline: Rise of automobiles and economic downturn during the Great Depression
  • Legacy: Once considered the "Coney Island of St. Paul."

Chronology

  • 1889: Wildwood Amusement Park opens on the shore of White Bear Lake
  • 1911: Minnetonka's Big Island Amusement Park closes
  • 1916: Wildwood is sold and renamed "White Bear Park"
  • 1927: New ownership takes over, adds new attractions like a roller coaster and swimming pool
  • 1932: Financial troubles force Wildwood Amusement Park to close permanently
  • 1938: Twin City Rapid Transit Corporation abandons the park; land is sold and developed for housing
  • 1951: Streetcar service to the area is discontinued

Then and Now

Erd Geist Gazebo

For decades, Wildwood Amusement Park entertained Twin Cities families on White Bear Lake's southern shore. Operating from 1899 to 1932, this "Coney Island of Saint Paul" featured roller coasters, dance pavilions, and lakeside attractions accessible by streetcar. Despite ownership changes and improvements, financial difficulties and the Great Depression forced closure. Little is known about the exact location of the former park, but much of the land is now filled with residential homes.
