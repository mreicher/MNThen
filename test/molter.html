Molter, Dorothy (1907 - 1986)

Creator: Matt Reicher Published: February 5, 2025

Dorothy Molter, a resident of what is now the Boundary Waters Canoe Area for more than 56 years (1930 - 1986), was once dubbed “the loneliest woman in America.” While her remote surroundings may have earned her this title, her life—filled with thousands of visitors to her Knife Lake wilderness resort each canoeing season—tells a decidedly different story.

Molter was born on May 6, 1907, in Arnold, Pennsylvania, to John "Cap" and Mattie Molter, and was the third of six children. Her mother passed away on May 22, 1915, and shortly after, Dorothy and her siblings were placed in a Cincinnati orphanage. This arrangement kept the children together, allowing their father to visit whenever he was on leave from the railroad. In 1919, “Cap” remarried, reunited with his children, and eventually moved the family to Chicago, Illinois.

In 1924, Dorothy entered Calumet High School, where she was active on the tennis, swimming, basketball, and rifle teams. After graduating, she attended Auburn Park Hospital Nursing School, intending to pursue a career in nursing. In the summer of 1930, she had her first visit to Knife Lake, accompanying her father, who had booked a fishing trip with his wife, brother, and two friends to Bill Berglund’s Isle of Pines resort. Dorothy substituted for one of his friends, who couldn’t make the trip due to changed plans.

The culture shock was immediate. As a big-city outsider, Dorothy was unfamiliar with the isolated surroundings she now found herself in. However, she was fearless and embraced challenges—quickly falling in love with the area. During this visit, she helped out at the resort, returning each season until, unable to find work as a nurse in Depression-era Chicago, she agreed to stay on and assist owner-operator Berglund year-round in 1934. She provided nursing care, managed his diabetes, and treated paddlers' first aid issues. Molter became the lodge’s owner when Berglund passed away in March 1948, as he had wished.

In 1948, lawmakers passed the Thye-Blatnik Act, which authorized the U.S. Forest Service to purchase resorts and private land within the future Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness. The goal of the legislation was to preserve the area’s beauty and compensate local counties for the loss of tax revenue from the lands. Molter was approached by the Forest Service, but declined to sell.

On December 17, 1949, President Harry S. Truman issued an executive order prohibiting aircraft from flying below 4,000 feet over the Superior Roadless Area (SRA), which included the Boundary Waters Canoe Area. The order was set to take effect on January 1, 1951. After years of relying on float planes to transport supplies to resorts, the new regulations forced owners—including Dorothy—to find other ways to receive those goods.

A small number of non-compliant local pilots continued to deliver supplies after the ban, but by 1952 the practice had ended. Molter, uncomfortable with the idea of portaging heavy supplies to her resort—especially pop bottles—turned to making her own root beer. This practice earned her the moniker “The Root Beer Lady,” and that, coupled with her dispute with the U.S. Forest Service over her resort land, made her a Northwoods celebrity.

The Saturday Evening Post featured an article about Dorothy Molter in its October 18, 1952, issue, titled "The Loneliest Woman in America." Ironically, despite her physical isolation, Molter was far from alone. Each season, between 6,000 and 7,000 people signed the guestbook at her resort, a testament to the steady stream of visitors she welcomed.

In 1964, the passage of the Wilderness Act formally established the Boundary Waters Canoe Area, making it illegal for Molter to continue running her resort. Supporters from across the country petitioned for her to be allowed to remain on her land, and in 1972, their efforts succeeded when Congress granted her lifetime tenancy. During the summers from 1976 to 1986, Dorothy and her helpers continued to brew her famous root beer, producing an average of over 10,000 bottles each season.

On December 18, 1986, Molter, the last full-time non-indigenous resident of the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness, died of natural causes at her resort. She is buried next to her mother in Pennsylvania. Her gravestone bears the epitaph “Nightingale of the Boundary Waters.”

Quick Facts

  • Name: Michael Allen "Bones" Hartzell
  • Born: February 22, 1947
  • Parents: Eugene and Delores Hartzell
  • Education: Washington High School, graduated 1965
  • Nickname Origin: "Bones" - given at Sylvan Recreation Center due to his skinniness
  • Military Service: Army, served in Korea during Vietnam War
  • Notable Role: Unofficial caretaker of Rice Street, North End neighborhood, St. Paul
  • Died: December 2, 2018
  • Age at Death: 71
  • Burial: Fort Snelling Cemetery

Chronology

  • February 22, 1947: Born in St. Paul
  • 1965: Graduated from Washington High School
  • Post-1965: Joined the Army, served in Korea during Vietnam War
  • 1977: Moved out of his parents' house and onto Rice Street
  • 1977-2018: Lived on Rice Street, maintaining sidewalks and business entryways
  • 1989: Assaulted while sleeping at a Super America
  • February 2017: 70th birthday celebration at Lonetti's Lounge, honored by state lawmakers
  • December 2, 2018: Passed away in hospice care after suffering from lung cancer and pneumonia
  • Post-December 2, 2018: Eight-hour-long funeral attended by an estimated 600 people
