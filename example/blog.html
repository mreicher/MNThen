Olson, Floyd B. (1891 - 1936)

Creator: Matt Reicher Published: July 17, 2023
Floyd B. Olson, a key figure in Minnesota politics during the Great Depression era, is remembered as a dedicated advocate for progressive policies and social justice. Serving as the 22nd Governor of Minnesota from 1931 until his untimely death in 1936, Olson's unwavering commitment to the well-being of working-class citizens and his dedication to economic reforms transformed the state's political landscape.

Born on November 13, 1891, in Minneapolis, Minnesota, Floyd Bjørnstjerne Olson grew up in a working-class family. He witnessed firsthand the struggles faced by ordinary Minnesotans during times of economic hardship, which fueled his passion for social justice and shaped his political ideology. Olson attended the University of Minnesota, where he honed his oratory skills and developed a deep understanding of the issues affecting working-class communities..

His political career began in 1919 when he was appointed special assistant to the Hennepin County Attorney. He became Hennepin County Attorney the following year. Known for his relentless pursuit of justice and his commitment to the rights of workers, he quickly gained recognition as a progressive leader. In 1930, he successfully ran for the office of governor, becoming the first member of the Farmer-Labor Party to hold the position.

As governor, Olson prioritized the welfare of ordinary Minnesotans and implemented a range of progressive policies. He staunchly advocated for labor rights, fighting for workers' rights to organize and collectively bargain. Olson's administration established the Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry, which aimed to protect workers' rights, ensure safe working conditions, and enforce labor laws.

Olson's commitment to economic reforms was exemplified by his support for public works programs and his efforts to alleviate the impact of the Great Depression on Minnesota's citizens. He championed the creation of the Minnesota Emergency Relief Administration, which provided employment opportunities and relief to those affected by the economic downturn. Additionally, his administration implemented the Unemployment Insurance Act, offering financial assistance to workers who had lost their jobs.

Beyond his focus on economic reforms, Olson was a vocal advocate for civil liberties and social justice. He supported legislation to combat discrimination and worked to secure equal rights for marginalized communities. Olson's commitment to racial equality was evident in his groundbreaking appointment of African Americans to prominent positions within his administration.

Tragically, Olson's time in office was cut short by illness. In 1936, he was diagnosed with stomach cancer and passed away on August 22 of the same year at the age of 44. His death was mourned by many who recognized his transformative leadership and his unwavering dedication to the welfare of working-class Minnesotans.

To honor Governor Floyd B. Olson's contributions to Minnesota's history and his progressive legacy, a memorial was dedicated to him at the Minnesota State Capitol in 1958. The Floyd B. Olson Memorial, located on the Capitol grounds, serves as a tribute to his leadership and the enduring impact of his policies. The memorial features a bronze statue of Olson, capturing his determined and resolute presence, and was crafted by Italian-American sculptors Carlo Brioschi and his son, Amerigo.

The Floyd B. Olson Memorial not only pays homage to the governor's legacy but also stands as a testament to the progressive movement he spearheaded. It symbolizes the ongoing importance of fighting for economic justice, workers' rights, and equal opportunities for all. The memorial serves as a source of inspiration for future generations of political leaders and activists, encouraging them to continue Olson's work in creating a more equitable and inclusive society.

Governor Floyd B. Olson's contributions to Minnesota's history and his progressive leadership continue to resonate today. His unwavering dedication to social justice, workers' rights, and economic reforms have left an indelible mark on the state's political landscape. The memorial at the Minnesota State Capitol stands as a lasting tribute to his enduring legacy and the impact of his visionary leadership.

Olson's commitment to progressive policies and his fight for the rights of working-class Minnesotans serve as a reminder that transformative change is possible through dedicated leadership and a belief in the power of social justice. His memory lives on as a beacon of hope and inspiration for those who strive for a more equitable and inclusive society.

Quick Facts

  • Name: Michael Allen "Bones" Hartzell
  • Born: February 22, 1947
  • Parents: Eugene and Delores Hartzell
  • Education: Washington High School, graduated 1965
  • Nickname Origin: "Bones" - given at Sylvan Recreation Center due to his skinniness
  • Military Service: Army, served in Korea during Vietnam War
  • Notable Role: Unofficial caretaker of Rice Street, North End neighborhood, St. Paul
  • Died: December 2, 2018
  • Age at Death: 71
  • Burial: Fort Snelling Cemetery

Chronology

  • February 22, 1947: Born in St. Paul
  • 1965: Graduated from Washington High School
  • Post-1965: Joined the Army, served in Korea during Vietnam War
  • 1977: Moved out of his parents' house and onto Rice Street
  • 1977-2018: Lived on Rice Street, maintaining sidewalks and business entryways
  • 1989: Assaulted while sleeping at a Super America
  • February 2017: 70th birthday celebration at Lonetti's Lounge, honored by state lawmakers
  • December 2, 2018: Passed away in hospice care after suffering from lung cancer and pneumonia
  • Post-December 2, 2018: Eight-hour-long funeral attended by an estimated 600 people
