Shootout at Lincoln Court Apartments (March 31, 1934)

Matt Reicher
Published: September 1, 2023

St. Paul's Lincoln Court Apartments, a charming brownstone complex located at 93–95 South Lexington Parkway, was once the setting of an epic gun battle between police, federal agents, and gangsters, including John Dillinger.

A seemingly minor police encounter with quiet residents of apartment 303 on March 31, 1934, escalated into another in a series of grand battles between law enforcement and the criminal underworld. A call to the authorities from Daisy Coffey, the building's owner, drew out America's "Public Enemy №1" and pushed the gangster menace back onto the front page of newspapers nationwide.

Less than two weeks earlier, on March 19, Dillinger and his girlfriend, Billie Frechette, had rented apartment 303 for $60 per month, using the alias Mr. and Mrs. Carl Hellman. Their intense need for secrecy immediately aroused the owner's suspicions. The couple always came in and out through the rear door of the building and kept the shades in their apartment closed. During the day, their apartment remained largely inactive; the only activity came at night.

On March 30, Coffey spoke with the FBI, expressing concerns about the Hellmans.

On a cold Saturday morning around 10:15 a.m., FBI Agent Rufus Coulter and Saint Paul Detective Henry Cummings knocked on the door of Apartment 303, while FBI Agent Rosser Nalls waited outside. When Frechette opened the door, the men asked to speak with Mr. Hellman. She hesitated, momentarily confused by the unfamiliar name—she had forgotten their alias at the worst possible time. Frechette quickly recovered, telling the officers he wasn't home. They asked if they could come in and talk with her. She nervously said she would have to get dressed first and quickly shut the door before waking Dillinger.

While the two officers awaited her return, a man walked up the stairs toward the apartment. Noticing law enforcement, he continued toward the front door. Agent Coulter followed, asking him what he was doing there. The man, reputed gangster Homer Van Meter, said he was a soap salesman. When asked about samples, Van Meter said he was getting them from his car, a green Ford Coupe parked nearby.

Agent Coulter followed Van Meter into the building's lobby. Realizing he was trapped, the gangster opened fire. He attempted to flee through the front entrance, but Agent Nalls, positioned outside, forced him back inside with return fire. A furious gun battle erupted. Witnesses described too many shots to count, although official reports never provided an exact tally.

Hearing gunfire, Dillinger cracked open his apartment door, pointed a Thompson submachine gun down the third-floor hallway, and sprayed bullets. The hail of gunfire forced the officers to take cover, allowing Dillinger and Frechette to leave through the building's unguarded rear entrance. Dillinger was struck in the upper left calf during the shooting, sustaining the only injury of the encounter.

While Dillinger covered her, Frechette ran to a nearby garage and grabbed the couple's new black Hudson sedan. She jumped in, and the two sped off. Once safely beyond the reach of authorities, they slowed to avoid drawing attention.

FBI Agent Nalls had shot holes into Van Meter's car tires to prevent his escape. When Van Meter found his car inoperable, he hijacked a garbage collector's horse, swapped his fedora for a hauler's cap, and rode away. The extraordinary image of a gangster fleeing on horseback through city streets would soon become part of gangland lore—though the FBI's official report states he seized a truck at gunpoint, neighborhood witnesses insisted on the horse.

The three later met at gangster Eddie Green's apartment on 3300 Fremont Avenue South in Minneapolis. Dillinger soon had his leg treated at the Minneapolis office of Dr. Clayton May before recuperating for a few days. He and Frechette then left Minnesota to visit Dillinger's father in Mooresville, Indiana.

In early 1934, a Ramsey County grand jury investigated allegations that the St. Paul police had allowed the city to become a haven for criminals. On March 31, the same day as the Lincoln Court Apartments shooting, the grand jury released its findings, stating the city did not have a gangster problem.

The St. Paul Daily News, under editor Howard Kahn and already at odds with city officials over their response to the gangster problem, expressed outrage. The paper noted that the grand jury's findings were released the same day officers engaged in a shootout with criminals at Lincoln Court Apartments.

It was another traumatic incident in a long series of violent crimes, and citizens had seen enough. Public sentiment, once mostly positive toward gangsters, shifted. People finally believed the city's violent criminal element needed to be removed.
