Summit Brewing Company (1986 - )

Creator: Matt Reicher
Published: October 8, 2023 Updated: June 8, 2025

Thirty-one-year-old St. Mary’s Hospital clinical social worker Mark Stutrud was at a career crossroads in the early 1980s. His professional life had stagnated, and the idea of continuing down his current path left him feeling unfulfilled. Stutrud decided he needed a change, either furthering his education or living out his dream of opening a small brewery. He chose the latter.

After conversations with noted industry insiders throughout the early 1980s, Stutrud began working full-time to get the brewery running. He spent his vacation learning the craft. Stutrud and his wife Margaret committed their life savings to the venture, and Stutrud enrolled in Chicago's Siebel Institute of Technology in May 1984 to learn the brewing trade. He completed market research, business development, and got the financing to open a 6,000-barrel-per-year brewery during this period. Stutrud incorporated the Summit Brewing Company (Summit) in 1984.

Commercial production began in September 1986 out of a 7,500-square-foot, former auto parts store at 2264 W. University Avenue in St. Paul. From day one, Summit focused on underrepresented beer styles. Its first product line was an English-style pale ale called Extra Pale Ale and a porter named Great Northern Porter.

On September 24, Summit sold its first two kegs to Johnny's Bar across the street. Initially struggling to find wholesalers, the company turned to restaurants and clubs that sold boutique wines, hoping they'd also consider boutique beers. The strategy worked, and within the first six months of business, Summit was self-distributing to fifty accounts.

The company remained committed to its local region despite entering the market at a time when large breweries dominated the national landscape. In its first three years, production increased from 1,500 to 3,700 barrels. Besides the two flagships, the product line expanded to include seasonal brews. Over time, the brewery's growth necessitated expansion. In July 1993, Summit leased a building across the alley at 731 Hampden Avenue and converted it into a bottling house. The addition expanded brewing capacity from 15,000 to 25,000 barrels per year.

By 1995, Summit had grown to be the fifth-largest microbrewery in the United States. It had outgrown its current space and began considering other locations. Company executives felt available Twin Cities structures were ill-suited for a brewery, so they looked for land to build a new brewery from the ground up.

On May 25, 1996, the St. Paul Port Authority announced it was considering selling 4.24 acres of undeveloped land at the Crosby Lake Business Park to Summit for one dollar. In exchange, the company committed to building a new million-dollar brewing facility that would allow them to increase yearly production capacity from 27,000 barrels to 70,000. Summit also agreed to hire at least ten additional employees.

The project took over three years to complete, more than doubling the company's brewing capacity. It began operation in October 1998. The new facility was the first new brewery built from the ground up in the state since before Prohibition.

In June 2013, the company expanded again, adding 7,632 square feet of cellar space with twelve 600-barrel fermentation vessels. This expansion increased their yearly production capacity to 240,000 barrels and included a canning facility that ended Summit's years as a bottle-only brewery. The additional capacity allowed Summit to diversify its offerings while maintaining quality, with the brewery's award-winning styles earning recognition locally, nationally, and internationally. Gone were the days of only the EPA and Great Northern Porter—though they remained flagship beers, they were joined by a continually growing lineup to entice beer drinkers with ever-discerning and ever-changing palates.

The industry had also changed dramatically. Gone were the days when Summit was one of a few local beers in a world of national brands. The 2011 Surly Bill, which allowed breweries to operate taprooms in the state, opened the doors to unprecedented local competition—from just a small number of craft brewers nationwide in 1986, there are now hundreds in Minnesota alone. This influx of new breweries and taprooms impacted Summit's bottom line, but the company adapted and continued to grow. It even joined the taproom game, re-opening its expanded Ratskeller to the public in July 2018. In 2020, despite increased competition and slower overall growth, Summit was named the largest brewery in the state by barrels produced annually.

From startup to industry leader, Summit had seen incredible growth over the years. The company was now an "establishment" brewery recognized as the pinnacle of achievement by other local and hyper-local brewers. Stutrud encouraged the competition with a caveat: ever customer-centric, he believed that as long as a hyper-focus on quality control remained top-of-mind, more local players brewing quality, sustainable beer could only benefit the consumer.

Quick Facts

  • Founder: Mark Stutrud
  • Company Name: Summit Brewing Company
  • Original Location: 2264 W. University Avenue, St. Paul
  • First Products: Extra Pale Ale and Great Northern Porter
  • Initial Brewery Size: 7,500 square feet
  • First Customer: Johnny's Bar
  • Current Location: Crosby Lake Business Park, St. Paul
  • Current Production Capacity: 240,000 barrels annually
  • Notable Achievement: Largest brewery in Minnesota by 2020

Summit Brewing: Then and Now

From its humble beginnings in a converted auto parts store to becoming Minnesota's largest brewery, Summit Brewing Company has been a pioneer in the craft beer movement. Founded by Mark Stutrud in 1986, Summit helped redefine Minnesota's beer culture and paved the way for hundreds of craft breweries that followed.
