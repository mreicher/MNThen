St. Paul's Downtown Ballpark (The "Pillbox") 1903-1909

Matt Reicher
Published: August 3, 2024
Updated: June 11, 2025

St. Paul's Downtown Ballpark, also known as "The Pillbox," was one of the city's earliest downtown professional ballparks. From 1903 through 1909, it served as the home field for the St. Paul Saints, occupying a city block just two blocks southeast of the new State Capitol building. The site was bounded by Robert Street to the northeast (first base side), 12th Street to the southeast (third base), Minnesota Street to the southwest (left field), and 13th Street—now Columbus Avenue—to the northwest (right field).

The ballpark's creation was born out of necessity. When Charles Comiskey's St. Paul Saints left for Chicago in 1900 to become the White Stockings (later shortened to the White Sox) in the newly formed American League, the city lost its Western League team. The Saints had been a Western League mainstay from 1895 through 1899. In 1901, George Lennon revived the franchise in St. Paul as a member of the American Association, and the team initially played at Lexington Park, southwest of the intersection of University and Lexington Avenues.

Lennon found the location problematic. While Lexington Park was suitable for baseball and could accommodate a crowd, it sat too far from downtown to serve as an effective home field. Poor fan attendance was costing him money, and he could not justify the increasing costs of fielding a winning team if the Saints remained there. American Association officials shared his concerns and insisted on relocating the team to a downtown ballpark within walking distance of area business districts. The ultimatum was clear: if Lennon failed to secure a downtown site, he would have to give up ownership, and the league would move the Saints franchise to Chicago.

In February 1903, Saints officials announced a second potential ballpark site in downtown St. Paul, on a city block bounded by Minnesota, Robert, and Twelfth Streets, and Summit Avenue. While the team met with city leaders to request building code variances, home and business owners who surrounded the location protested, believing a new ballpark would harm their neighborhood and lower property values. Two key compromises were made: no Sunday games would be played, and no advertisements would be permitted on the outside fence. On May 8, 1903, Mayor Robert Smith signed a resolution approving the downtown ballpark. However, surrounding property owners filed a building injunction. In June, the court dismissed it, clearing the way for construction.

The resulting facility was a dramatic departure from Lexington Park. While the old park held 10,000 fans, the much smaller downtown venue seated only between 2,000 and 3,000. The field was situated on a 330 ft by 250 ft block, measuring just 210 ft from home plate to the right foul pole and 280 ft to the left field corner. A ball hit over the right field fence was considered a ground-rule double, though poles marked where a ball clearing the fence would count as a home run instead. Triples were virtually non-existent. Fans in the grandstand and bleachers sat no more than 10 or 20 feet from the baselines and were protected by netting. Home plate, which faced northwest, was so close to the grandstand that it could not be seen clearly by everyone attending. Because of the close quarters, players struggled to catch foul balls, and the ballpark was commonly known as the "Pillbox."

The ballpark opened to the public on July 20, 1903. In the first game, the Saints beat the Minneapolis Millers 11 to 2 in front of more than 4,500 people—well beyond capacity. The park remained the home field for the Saints through the 1909 season, except for Sunday games. Only one Sunday game was played there: on May 12, 1907, "the most orderly crowd that ever attended a baseball game in this city" came out to see the Saints beat Indianapolis 6-5.

While the Saints were the primary tenants, the Pillbox served a broader role in St. Paul's sports landscape. The ballpark regularly served as home to the all-Black St. Paul Gophers (also called the St. Paul Colored Gophers). The facility also operated year-round, hosting speed skating competitions during winter months and professional wrestling matches in summer. Following the 1909 season, the ballpark was abandoned. The Saints returned to Lexington Park, and the downtown site was sold to the St. Paul Bread Company in late 1910.
