St. Paul's Downtown Ballpark (The "Pillbox") 1903-1909

Creator: Matt Reicher
Published: August 3, 2024 Updated: June 11, 2025

St. Paul's Downtown Ballpark, also known as "The Pillbox," was one of the city's earliest downtown professional ballparks. It served as the home field for the St. Paul Saints, from 1903 through 1909. The ballpark was located two blocks southeast of the new State Capitol building, occupying a city block bounded by Robert Street to the northeast (first base side), 12th Street to the southeast (third base), Minnesota Street to the southwest (left field), and 13th Street (now Columbus Avenue) to the northwest (right field).

When Charles Comiskey's St. Paul Saints left for Chicago in 1900 to become the White Stockings (later shortened to White Sox) in the newly formed American League, the city lost its Western League team. The Saints had been a Western League mainstay from 1895-1899. In 1901, clothing merchant George Lennon revived the Saints franchise in St. Paul, this time as a member of the American Association. The team initially played its games at Lexington Park, located southwest of the intersection of University and Lexington Avenues.

Lennon felt that location was problematic. While Lexington Park was suitable for baseball and could accommodate a suitable crowd, it was located too far from downtown to serve as an effective home field for the team. He was losing money due to poor fan attendance at Lexington Park. He couldn't justify the increasing costs of fielding a winning team if the Saints remained there.

American Association officials shared Lennon's concerns about Lexington Park. They insisted on relocating the team to a downtown ballpark within walking distance of area business districts. They believed this would improve the Saints' financial success. The ultimatum was clear: if Lennon failed to secure a downtown site, he would have to give up ownership, and the league would move the Saints franchise to Chicago.

In February 1903, Saints officials announced a second potential ballpark site in downtown St. Paul. The site was on a city block bounded by Minnesota, Robert, Twelfth Streets, and Summit Avenue. While the team met with city leaders to request building code variances, home and business owners who surrounded the location protested. They believed a new ballpark would harm their neighborhood and lower property values.

To address their potential neighbors' concerns, two key compromises were made: no Sunday games would be played, and no advertisements would be permitted on the outside fence surrounding the ballpark. On May 8, 1903, mayor Robert Smith signed a resolution approving the downtown ballpark. However, surrounding property owners filed a building injunction against the park. In June, the court dismissed this injunction, clearing the way for construction. With legal hurdles overcome, work on the ballpark began.

While Lexington Park held 10,000 fans, the much smaller downtown park only seated between 2,000 and 3,000. The field was situated on a 330 ft x 250 ft block. It measured only 210 ft from home plate to the right foul pole and 280 ft to the left field corner. A ball hit over the right field fence was considered a ground-rule double. There were poles that marked where balls hit over the fence would be a home run instead of a double. Triples were virtually non-existent.

Fans in the grandstand and bleachers sat no more than 10 or 20 feet from the base lines and were protected by netting. Home plate, which faced northwest, was so close to the grandstand that it couldn't be seen clearly by everyone attending. Because of the close quarters, it was challenging for players to catch foul balls. Due to its small size and dimensions, the ballpark was commonly known as the "Pillbox."

The ballpark opened to the public on July 20, 1903. In the first game, the Saints beat the Minneapolis Millers, 11 to 2 in front of more than 4,500 people—well beyond capacity. The park remained the home field for the Saints through the 1909 season, except for Sunday games. Only one Sunday game was played there. In that May 12, 1907 game, "the most orderly crowd that ever attended a baseball game in this city" came out to see the Saints beat Indianapolis 6-5.

While the Saints were the primary tenants, the Pillbox served a broader role in St. Paul's sports landscape. The ballpark regularly served as home to the all-Black St. Paul Gophers (also called the St. Paul Colored Gophers). The facility also operated year-round, hosting speed skating competitions during winter months and professional wrestling matches in summer. Following the 1909 season, the ballpark was abandoned. The Saints returned to Lexington Park, and the downtown site was sold to the St. Paul Bread Company in late 1910.

Quick Facts

  • Name: St. Paul's Downtown Ballpark, "The Pillbox"
  • Location: Bounded by Robert St, 12th St, Minnesota St, and 13th St (now E. Columbus Ave)
  • Team: St. Paul Saints
  • League: American Association
  • Operated: 1903-1909
  • Capacity: 2,000-3,000 spectators
  • Size: 330 ft x 250 ft block
  • Dimensions: 210 ft to right field, 280 ft to left field
  • Notable Feature: Extremely small size, leading to unique playing conditions

Timeline

  • 1901: George Lennon revives the St. Paul Saints franchise
  • 1902: Lennon begins efforts to build a downtown ballpark
  • February 1903: Saints announce potential downtown ballpark site
  • May 8, 1903: Mayor Robert Smith approves downtown ballpark resolution
  • June 1903: Court dismisses building injunction, allowing construction
  • July 20, 1903: Downtown Ballpark opens to the public
  • May 12, 1907: Only Sunday game played at the ballpark
  • 1909: Final season for the Saints at Downtown Ballpark
  • Post-1909: Ballpark closed and land sold to St. Paul Bread Company for $50,000

Downtown Ballpark: Then and Now

From 1903-1909, this compact downtown ballpark served as home to the St. Paul Saints, known affectionately as "The Pillbox" due to its small dimensions. The field's right field foul pole stood just 210 feet from home plate, with left field at 280 feet. Today, the site is occupied by Government buildings near Minnesota and Robert Streets, with no visible remnants of the historic ballpark.
