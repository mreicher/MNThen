State Capitol Rathskeller

Twenty-two layers of paint, wartime hysteria, and Prohibition politics — the hidden German beer hall beneath Minnesota's Capitol

Matt Reicher October 31, 2024 Updated July 2, 2025 St. Paul, MN

On January 2, 1905, Minnesota's third state capitol building opened its doors to the public, one day before the 34th Legislature convened. Designed by Cass Gilbert and immediately deemed the crown jewel of Minnesota, the stunning structure held a hidden gem: a German-themed dining room in the basement with vaulted ceilings, hand-painted murals, and German-language inscriptions on the walls. Built as a refuge from heated policy arguments upstairs, those painted mottoes and vaulted ceilings would outlast two wars, Prohibition, and fifty years hidden under layers of paint until someone finally decided to rediscover them.

I

A Council Cellar in the Capitol

Gilbert modeled the Rathskeller after a traditional German "Council Cellar," a deliberate choice. In European city halls, these basement spaces served as gathering places where hierarchy could be set aside. Chief decorator Elmer Garnsey leaned into the contrast, transforming cold stone into something organic and lived-in. He used warm ochres and earthy reds, covering vaulted ceilings with "grotesque" murals filled with grapevines and mythical creatures. The crowning touch was the text: 29 hand-painted German mottoes extolling drinking and good cheer. These weren't merely decorative. They anchored Minnesota's largest immigrant group in the state's most important building, and Gilbert's documented relationships with St. Paul's German community leaders suggest the design was as much cultural statement as architectural one.

II

Wartime Erasure

In 1917, as anti-German sentiment reached fever pitch, Governor J.A.A. Burnquist, leader of the Minnesota Commission of Public Safety, wielded wartime authority to deem the room's German heritage a threat to "Americanism." Garnsey's 29 slogans were painted over, burying intentional storytelling under industrial whitewash. Though the Commission disbanded in 1919, the erasure remained. The mottoes stayed hidden for more than a decade.

III

Prohibition & Compromise

By 1930, anti-German sentiment had waned. Governor Theodore Christianson called for restoration. But the nation was in Prohibition. Temperance groups balked — if the language returned, the drinking references could not. Christianson compromised. "Better tipsy than feverish" became "Temperance is a virtue of men." "Cheerful disposition and noble wine" became "noble food." Once again, the Rathskeller changed not because something was broken, but because politics demanded it.

IV

Fifty Years of Forgetting

In the late 1930s, cafeteria equipment replaced the room's original character. Steam tables and chrome serving lines displaced elegance. Workers plastered over giant windows and sections of the vaulted ceiling. Garnsey's murals and 1930s mottoes were painted over again. For fifty years, the room remained culturally unidentifiable. Generations ate lunch beneath those vaulted ceilings unaware of what lay beneath the paint. By 1988, it was a shell of itself. Water damage had eaten through plaster. Asbestos covered the ceiling. Food fumes and cigar smoke had left thick, discolored film on the walls.

That year, a conversation began about restoring the Capitol's original amenities. Architectural conservator Robert A. Furhoff, a historic paint specialist from Chicago, was brought in. Armed with a scalpel, he spent months chipping through eighty years of paint, documenting every original layer. Just as secrets were being unearthed, funding ran out.

V

The Restoration

In 1996, the legislature appropriated $1.2 million for restoration, then another $1.04 million. In May 1998, Carolyn Kompelien and Dan Tarnoveanu led a team of preservationists into the basement with scalpels and tweezers. They scraped away twenty-two layers of paint and grime — a journey backward through time. Beneath the 1930s drab and 1917 whitewash, Garnsey's 1905 vision re-emerged. The mythical animals, grapevines, and vibrant ochre tones were uncovered inch by inch. Even the room's quirks survived: one eagle's beak points differently from the rest, and on one shield, a quarter is rounded where another is squared.

The state restored the original 1905 mottoes in their true form, undoing Prohibition edits and returning drinking references to the walls. When the Rathskeller Café reopened in 2000, it stood exactly as Gilbert and Garnsey intended. For two decades, it became what it was designed to be. On March 17, 2020, the pandemic closed it. It reopened in 2022.

Today, it stands as proof of what happens when political will aligns with historical commitment. Twenty-two layers of paint, wartime hysteria, temperance politics, and fifty years of forgetting couldn't erase what a careful architect and decorator created. The space endures as a window into what Minnesota's largest immigrant community contributed to the state's most important building — and what the state lost by trying to hide it.

Partial restoration attempted. Prohibition forces edits — drinking references replaced.

Key Figures

Cass Gilbert

Architect of the Minnesota State Capitol. Modeled the Rathskeller after a traditional German Council Cellar, with documented ties to St. Paul's German community leadership.

Elmer Garnsey

Chief decorator who filled the vaulted ceilings with "grotesque" murals of grapevines and mythical creatures, and hand-painted all 29 German mottoes.

Gov. J.A.A. Burnquist

Ordered the German mottoes painted over in 1917 as leader of the Minnesota Commission of Public Safety, citing a threat to "Americanism."

Robert A. Furhoff

Chicago-based architectural conservator who began uncovering the original layers in 1988, documenting every stratum before funding ran out.

Carolyn Kompelien & Dan Tarnoveanu

Led the 1998 restoration team that removed 22 layers of paint with scalpels and tweezers, returning Garnsey's original vision to the light.
