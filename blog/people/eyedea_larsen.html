Larsen, Micheal "Eyedea" (1981–2010)

From battle rap champion to genre-bending artist — the fiercely independent MC who became a Twin Cities icon

Matt Reicher September 4, 2023 Updated June 7, 2025 St. Paul, MN

Micheal David Larsen was born on November 9, 1981, in Fayetteville, North Carolina, to seventeen-year-old Kathy Averill. Of Irish and Lebanese descent, he moved with his mother to St. Paul as an infant. He grew up in a creative household that nurtured his early artistic impulses, eventually becoming a profoundly gifted battle rapper and hip-hop artist.

I

Early Life & Artistic Roots

His gifts were visible almost immediately: he began writing poems in kindergarten, and at six years old he and his friends formed a fictitious band with makeshift wooden instruments, entertaining friends and family. As he moved into adolescence, Larsen's fierce independence and non-conformist sensibilities often put him at odds with authority—a spirit that would later define his refusal to obey hip-hop's commercial rules.

II

The Battle Rap Prodigy

Larsen attended Highland Park Senior High School in St. Paul, where his lyrical abilities deepened and began to set him apart. He started rapping at fourteen, and by seventeen he had won the 1999 Scribble Jam freestyle battle. In 2000, at age nineteen, he added both HBO's Blaze Battle and the Rock Steady Anniversary competition to his résumé, establishing himself as one of the era's most formidable freestyle MCs.

III

The Independent Path

In 2001, Eyedea & Abilities released their debut studio album, First Born, which showcased Larsen's philosophical and introspective lyrical style; the single "Big Shots" later appeared on the soundtrack for Tony Hawk's Pro Skater 4, exposing him to a global audience. Following this success, Larsen channeled the highly skilled material he had been compiling since his mid-teens into a solo project. In 2002, he released the entirely self-produced album The Many Faces of Oliver Hart under his solo alias, which quickly became a cult favorite in underground hip-hop circles.

Persistent rumors long suggested that major labels sought to sign him following his televised battle victories—including widespread stories of an offer from P. Diddy's Bad Boy Records, which Larsen later denied. He consciously bypassed the commercial mainstream, choosing instead to release his music through Rhymesayers Entertainment and, later, his own imprint, Crushkill Recordings, to maintain complete creative control. During this incredibly active period, he was also part of the celebrated underground supergroup The Orphanage alongside Slug, Aesop Rock, Blueprint, Sage Francis, and Illogic, before releasing the second Eyedea & Abilities album, E&A, in 2004.

IV

Beyond Hip-Hop

Larsen's commitment to art over medium led him to step away from the underground hip-hop spotlight in 2006. He explored punk rock with the band Carbon Carousel (EP: The Some of All Things, or: The Healing Power of Scab Picking) and improvisational jazz-rap with the ensemble Face Candy (album: This Is Where We Were). During this period he also collaborated with artist Louis N. LaPierre on a limited poetry and art book, Once A Queen… Always A Creep (2007), of which only eighty copies were produced and sold during tour stops. These experiments met varied reactions from his fanbase, and the era was marked by personal struggles as he grappled with depression, anxiety, and severe insomnia.

V

By the Throat — A Final Masterpiece

Eyedea & Abilities reunited in 2008 to create their third and final album together. By the Throat was released in July 2009, and became their most critically acclaimed work, reaching #9 on Billboard's Heatseekers Albums chart. It represented the culmination of Larsen's years of experimentation, infusing the duo's signature sound with the raw, live-band energy of his side projects. Tragically, it would be their last collaboration.

VI

A Tragic End & Lasting Legacy

On October 16, 2010, at age twenty-eight, Larsen was found dead in his St. Paul apartment. The Ramsey County Medical Examiner ruled the death an accidental opiate toxicity causing respiratory depression. Struggling with chronic insomnia and depression, he had taken a fatal combination of prescription medications. In response to public misconceptions, his mother emphasized that this was an isolated accident—not the result of habitual drug use.

The local music scene rallied to honor him. On November 9, 2010—what would have been his twenty-ninth birthday—a capacity crowd filled First Avenue's Mainroom for a memorial celebration featuring a who's-who of Twin Cities underground hip-hop. Commemorative tributes have continued in the years since: a park bench in St. Paul's Cherokee Park was dedicated on October 16, 2011, and a mural was painted on the side of his grandfather's Como Avenue auto garage in October 2018.

Larsen left behind thousands of pages of unpublished poetry and dozens of unreleased songs, some of which have been released posthumously. His story was chronicled in the 2015 documentary The World Has No Eyedea.

Eyedea's significance rests not only in his staggering technical skill, but in his absolute refusal to treat underground credibility as currency. He turned down major-label deals, walked away from battle-rap fame to explore punk and jazz, and never allowed commercial pressure to dictate his art.

Key Figures

Kathy Averill

Eyedea's mother, who raised him as a single parent from age seventeen. After his death, she publicly clarified the circumstances to combat misinformation.

DJ Abilities (Gregory Keltner)

Musical partner and collaborator in Eyedea & Abilities. Together they released three critically acclaimed albums spanning nearly a decade.

Oliver Hart

Larsen's solo alias, under which he self-produced The Many Faces of Oliver Hart — a cult favorite recorded largely before he graduated high school.

Larsen, Micheal "Eyedea" (1981–2010): Then & Now

Micheal "Eyedea" Larsen rose from a poetry-writing child in St. Paul to become one of underground hip-hop's most revered figures. After winning the prestigious Scribble Jam battle at 17, he rejected major label offers to stay independent, crafting deeply philosophical music that transcended genres. His sudden death at 28 shocked Minnesota's music community, but memorials like his Cherokee Park bench and Como Avenue mural ensure his legacy endures.
