Humphrey, Hubert H. (1911 – 1978)

Matt Reicher
Published: July 14, 2023

Hubert H. Humphrey was a giant in American politics during the mid-20th century, and his impact on the nation's history is hard to overstate. People called him "The Happy Warrior" because of his upbeat personality and his fierce dedication to civil rights and progressive values. But Humphrey was more than just optimistic. He was stubborn, relentless, and guided by a deep moral compass. He became a key figure in shaping domestic policy and fought for social justice long before it was politically safe.

Born May 27, 1911, in Wallace, South Dakota, Hubert Horatio Humphrey came from a modest family that struggled through the Depression. After high school, he went to the University of Minnesota and discovered a passion for political activism and reform. His early years—particularly helping his father keep their family pharmacy, Humphrey Drug Company, afloat during the Great Depression—gave him real empathy for working-class Americans. It also convinced him that government could, and should, aspire to make life fairer.

Humphrey's political career began taking shape in the 1940s when he became mayor of Minneapolis. Under his watch, the city became known nationwide as a model for advancing civil rights and fair employment practices, establishing the nation's first municipal fair employment law. Then came 1948. At the Democratic National Convention that year, Humphrey delivered a passionate, fiery speech pushing for a strong civil rights plank in the party platform. It was so controversial that dozens of Southern delegates walked out in protest. Historians now see that moment as the turning point that pushed the Democratic Party toward civil rights and racial equality. Humphrey took a real risk—his career was just starting to take off—but his courage helped define the party's future stance on human rights.

Later that year, he won election to the U.S. Senate and kept pushing for civil rights legislation. His tireless efforts earned him respect from civil rights leaders across the country. Humphrey became crucial in the Senate's fight for the Civil Rights Act of 1964. He worked across party lines—especially with Republican Minority Leader Everett Dirksen—to break a 57-day filibuster, the longest in U.S. history, and get the bill passed. That legislative victory remains one of the most significant in modern American history.

Humphrey's influence expanded when President Lyndon B. Johnson chose him as Vice President in 1964. He didn't just champion progressive policies—he helped advance the major pillars of Johnson's Great Society, including the Voting Rights Act of 1965 and War on Poverty programs. Behind the scenes, Humphrey often served as Johnson's key advocate and Senate mediator, navigating complicated negotiations on Medicare, education funding, and anti-poverty initiatives.

But Humphrey's national ambitions got tangled up in the Vietnam War. His loyalty to Johnson meant he had to support the administration's war policy, even as public opposition mounted. This put Humphrey in an impossible spot, trying to balance loyalty with conscience. Eventually, late in the 1968 campaign, he publicly broke from Johnson and called for a bombing halt. The move helped him close the polling gap, but it came too late to change the election's outcome.

Though he lost to Richard Nixon in 1968, Humphrey remained a respected public figure. He returned to the U.S. Senate in 1971 and continued pushing progressive policies—health care reform, arms control, expanded education funding. Even while battling terminal cancer in his final years, colleagues remembered Humphrey as one of the most energetic, hopeful, and kind members of Congress. He always made time for younger legislators and constituents. He served until his death on January 13, 1978.

Decades later, his contributions were honored on August 4, 2012, when a seven-foot bronze statue of Humphrey was unveiled outside the Minnesota State Capitol. Designed by Chicago-based artists Anna Koh-Varilla and Jeffrey Varilla, the statue shows Humphrey with outstretched arms and a wide, unmistakable smile. A bench and engraved stone panels with his quotes accompany the memorial. At the dedication, attendees—including Vice President Joe Biden, former President Bill Clinton, Governor Mark Dayton, and members of the Humphrey family—reflected on his lifelong fight for equality, compassion, and justice.

Hubert Humphrey's legacy as the "Happy Warrior" remains. He is considered a civil rights champion, a voice for the marginalized, and a model for how optimism and conviction can shape a nation's moral direction. His blend of idealism and political courage still inspires activists, public servants, and ordinary Americans who believe in building a more equitable society. Decades after his death, Humphrey's work echoes in the laws we live under and in the values we keep striving to uphold.
