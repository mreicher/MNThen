Olson, Floyd B. (1891 – 1936)

Matt Reicher
Published: July 17, 2023

Floyd B. Olson was a central figure in Minnesota politics during the Great Depression, remembered as a fierce advocate for working-class Minnesotans and progressive change. As the 22nd governor of Minnesota from 1931 until his untimely death in 1936, Olson fundamentally reshaped the state's political landscape. His commitment to economic reforms, labor rights, and social justice left a mark that outlasted his brief time in office.

Born November 13, 1891, in Minneapolis, Olson came from a working-class family. The son of poor Scandinavian immigrants, he watched everyday Minnesotans struggle through tough economic times, and those experiences shaped his drive to improve life for the state's working people. After a year at the University of Minnesota and earning his law degree, he worked as a prosecutor and quickly earned a reputation as a progressive with a genuine passion for justice. Before reaching the governor's mansion, Olson served as Hennepin County Attorney, where people knew him for his aggressive stance on crime and his unwavering commitment to fairness.

Olson's breakthrough came in 1930 when he became the first Farmer-Labor Party member to win the governorship. The victory marked a turning point for Minnesota politics—a new political force had arrived, one that would challenge corporate interests while championing social and economic justice. Once in office, he wasted no time fighting for workers' rights. He helped pass laws supporting labor unions and protecting workplace safety. Among his achievements was strengthening the Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry, which ensured workers' rights were respected and workplaces remained safe.

The Great Depression tested Olson's leadership, and he responded with fierce advocacy for economic reform. He pushed hard for public works programs that created jobs and brought relief to the unemployed. Under his watch, the Minnesota Emergency Relief Administration was established to help those hit hardest by economic collapse. Olson also championed unemployment insurance legislation, though Minnesota's act wouldn't pass until 1938, two years after his death. These public welfare programs earned him widespread respect, though they also drew fierce opposition from conservative business leaders who viewed his policies as dangerously radical.

Olson's commitment to justice extended well beyond economic issues. He became a vocal advocate for civil liberties and frequently spoke out against discrimination. However, his relationship with labor was complex—during the 1934 Minneapolis Truckers Strike, he reluctantly declared martial law, a move that alienated both strikers and business leaders. His administration also fought for public education reforms, working to improve school access for Minnesota's children regardless of their background or economic status.

His progressive vision didn't stop at state borders. In 1934, Olson called for a national program of public works and government intervention to stimulate job creation during the Depression. These proposals laid crucial groundwork for many federal New Deal programs that President Franklin D. Roosevelt would later champion. Olson's vision for government involvement in economic recovery was remarkably forward-thinking, establishing him as a key figure in the 1930s progressive movement.

Tragically, illness cut short Olson's promising political career. In 1936, doctors diagnosed him with cancer. He died at just 44 years old. His death left an enormous void in Minnesota politics—the state had lost a true voice for the people and a tireless advocate for social change. Olson's early passing also marked the decline of the Farmer-Labor Party's influence in state politics, as the party struggled to maintain momentum without his dynamic leadership.

To honor his contributions, a memorial statue of Olson was dedicated at the Minnesota State Capitol. Located on the Capitol grounds, the bronze statue created by Italian-American sculptors Carlo and Amerigo Brioschi stands as a tribute to his determined spirit and his enduring impact on Minnesota's political landscape.

But the memorial represents more than just Olson's life—it symbolizes the progressive movement he helped lead. His work advancing workers' rights, economic justice, and civil liberties continues inspiring political leaders and activists today. Olson's fight for a more fair and equal society serves as a powerful reminder of what his committed leadership achieved.

Governor Olson's impact on Minnesota's political history is still felt today. His focus on economic reforms and social justice laid the foundation for many of the state's future policies. The memorial at the State Capitol stands as a testament to his lasting influence and to the belief that meaningful change is possible when you're willing to fight for what's right.
