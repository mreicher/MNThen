Johnson, Marjorie "The Blue Ribbon Baker" (1919 – 2025)

Creator: Matt Reicher
Published: November 26, 2025 Updated: November 26, 2025

Standing just 4 feet 8 inches tall, Marjorie Eleanora Johnson (née Holtby) may have been small in stature, but her spirit—and her baking prowess—made her a giant. Known affectionately as Minnesota’s “Blue Ribbon Baker,” she amassed thousands of awards at state fairs and national contests over more than five decades. Known for her bubbly personality, boundless energy, and infectious enthusiasm, she charmed talk-show hosts, delighted audiences, and inspired home bakers across the country.

Johnson was born in Minneapolis on August 9, 1919, to John Jr. and Esther Holtby. The fourth of six daughters, she grew up in the Jordan neighborhood on the city’s North Side. While her sisters were quiet and reserved, she was effervescent and vivacious—an extrovert among introverts, as she later described herself. She graduated from North High School in 1937 and went on to study Home Economics at the University of Minnesota.

Her mother was a fabulous baker, and helping her in the kitchen drew her into the craft. She recalled her first foray into baking at age 8, making a chocolate cake with her mother. Hearing her mom offer a slice to a family friend—“Oh Doris, you must come in and have some of this cake Marjorie made. You’ll never believe how great it is!”—was all she needed. She was hooked for a lifetime. Those early experiences, combined with watching her mother bake everything from scratch during the Depression, taught her resourcefulness and a love for homemade baked goods.

Marjorie graduated with a Bachelor of Science in Home Economics, majoring in Foods in Business and minoring in Journalism—where she also met her future husband, LeRoy “Lee” Johnson, then enrolled in dental school. Lee was quiet and reserved, a striking contrast to Marjorie’s high-energy personality. They married in 1946 and had three children: Marilee, Marleen, and Steven. In 1968, they purchased a split-level home in Robbinsdale, where Marjorie would live for the rest of her life.

Until age 55, Johnson baked solely for family and friends. In 1974, she entered four baked items in the Minnesota State Fair, winning two second-place ribbons and one third-place ribbon. This initial success gave her the encouragement needed to pursue competitive baking more seriously. Over the following decades, she refined her techniques, often testing recipes multiple times and working late into the night—sometimes until 2 a.m.—to perfect a new pie or cake.

Johnson became a consistent champion at the Fair, establishing herself as a top contender across multiple categories—pies, cakes, breads, cookies, even preserves. But her true breakout came not from a ribbon, but from a microphone. In 1993, she appeared as a guest on Joe Soucheray’s Garage Logic radio show—live from the Fair—and instantly captivated listeners with her rapid-fire wit, self-deprecating humor, and unmistakable “munchkin” voice. She became the show’s opening-day tradition for over thirty years, and from that modest stage, her fame soared.

Her first national television appearance was in November 1996 on The Rosie O’Donnell Show. Between then and 2002, Johnson appeared twelve times as a featured guest. O’Donnell once said of her, “The first time I met her, I couldn’t believe this adorable petite woman could have such a dynamic personality. She’s tough, hysterical, talks a mile a minute, has the biggest heart of anyone I know, and I just wanted to take her home with me.” Her signature red dress—worn every year at the Fair and on nearly every TV appearance—became part of her legend.

Johnson was a recurring guest for two seasons on The Wayne Brady Show (2002–2004), appearing more than twenty times, and reunited with Rosie on The View in 2006. Her most improbable—and beloved—national run began in January 2007 on The Tonight Show with Jay Leno, where she appeared twenty-one times. Leno sent her as a roving correspondent to events as wildly varied as the Emmys, the NBA All-Star Game, the X-Games, and the 2007 Las Vegas Bike Fest—where she famously discovered that you could, in fact, dunk ginger snaps in Jack Daniels.

Though she charmed audiences from coast to coast, Johnson never let fame eclipse her roots. She remained deeply loyal to the Minnesota State Fair, local television stations (including KARE‑11 and Twin Cities Live), and her neighbors in Robbinsdale—where Mayor Regan Murphy declared April 30, 2007, “Marjorie Johnson Day.” In 2009, she added another milestone: winning Martha Stewart’s inaugural national pie contest with her pecan-toffee pie, beating 161 competitors and later demonstrating the recipe live on The Martha Stewart Show.

At the heart of Marjorie’s world was her family. In 2013, her beloved husband of sixty-seven years, LeRoy “Lee” Johnson, passed away at the age of 95. She described him affectionately as a “blue ribbon person,” affirming that their marriage was her favorite relationship. Six years later, in 2019, to celebrate her 100th birthday, she returned to national television as a guest on The Kelly Clarkson Show, where she was interviewed alongside Jay Leno—one final joyful bow on the big stage.

On October 30, 2025, with her children by her side, Johnson passed away in hospice care. She was 106.

While her media fame spanned decades, Johnson’s true legacy was forged at the Minnesota State Fair. Over nearly 50 years of competitive baking, she amassed an estimated more than 2,500 ribbons—including over 1,000 blue ribbons at the Fair alone. She visited the Fair annually until 2022, always bringing tins labeled in masking tape (“M. Johnson – RETURN PLEASE!”), always greeting judges, vendors, and fans with equal warmth.

Fans remember her not for trophies—but for her dynamic personality, her rapid-fire train of thought, her refusal to dwell on setbacks, and her insistence that joy is a choice, baked fresh every day. She radiated kindness and enthusiasm for life, and never denied herself a treat or two—especially if it came with whipped cream and a side of laughter.

Quick Facts

  • Born: August 9, 1919, Minneapolis, Minnesota
  • Died: October 30, 2025, Robbinsdale, Minnesota
  • Known For: Record-setting Minnesota State Fair baker; frequent guest on national television; author of The Road to Blue Ribbon Baking
  • Signature Look: Always wore a red dress and pinned blue ribbons to her lapel
  • Family: Married LeRoy “Lee” Johnson (1946–2013); three children, four grandchildren
  • Legacy: Embodiment of joy, resilience, and homemade excellence—proving that greatness comes in all sizes
